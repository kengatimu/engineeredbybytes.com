Concept F — six variations on label + description

Each variation uses the same eight pains from the WEBSITE_V2_PAIN_TAXONOMY. Pick the typography + structure that feels most like EBB — premium, banker-credible, and bespoke without being gimmicky.

F1

Classic editorial list

UPPERCASE label (crimson) — serif description on the same row. Clean horizontal rules between items. The closest direct refinement of the original Concept F.

The Problem

Your stack has the same problems as every other bank's.

  • Timeouts Your customers see the outage before your dashboards do.
  • Silent reversals A reversal fails. Finance finds the gap on Friday.
  • Week-long audits A single regulator query consumes your engineering team for five days.
  • Six-month corridors Every new operator is a fresh six-month integration project.
  • Midnight deploys Releases at 11pm. Rollbacks at 2am. Repeat next sprint.
  • Breach surface Every new integration is a new attack vector.
  • Manual reconciliation Weekends in spreadsheets. Four thousand rows. Manual matching.
  • Engineering drain Your team is in maintenance mode, not shipping products.

Why this works: instantly scannable. Label gives the category, description delivers the moment. Risk: may feel formal — could read as documentation.

F2

Two-column glossary

Right-aligned monospace term on the left, serif definition on the right. Reads like a reference appendix or technical glossary. Highly structured, almost academic in feel.

The Problem

Eight problems your engineering team carries every week.

TIMEOUT
Your customers see the outage before your dashboards do. Twitter is faster than monitoring.
SILENT REVERSAL
A reversal fails. No alert fires. Finance discovers it three days later during reconciliation.
AUDIT QUERY
"Show me Q3 reversals by reason code." Five dashboards. Five days. Every quarter.
CORRIDOR BUILD
A new operator is a six-month project. New protocol, new AML, new audit trail — every time.
DEPLOY RISK
Releases at 11pm because daytime is too risky. Rollbacks at 2am. On-call paged.
ATTACK SURFACE
Every new integration adds another vector. Ten operators = ten places encryption can be wrong.
RECONCILIATION
Month-end. Spreadsheets. Four thousand rows. Manual matching. Weekends consumed.
CAPACITY DRAIN
Eighty percent of engineering time goes to maintenance. Twenty percent to the products that win business.

Why this works: looks like an authoritative reference document — banker / auditor will recognise the structure instantly. Risk: on mobile the two-column layout collapses to stacked, losing some of its identity.

F4

Monospace tag + serif body

Technical-spec / changelog aesthetic. Each pain has a monospace tag (like a code identifier) above a serif description. Crimson hover-accent on the left rule. Mixes the credibility of an engineering doc with the polish of editorial type.

The Problem

Eight problems we hear in every conversation.

  • timeout.outage Your customers see the outage before your dashboards do.
  • reversal.silent A reversal fails silently. Finance discovers the gap three days later.
  • audit.weeklong One regulator query consumes your engineering team for five days. Every quarter.
  • corridor.6mo Every new operator is a six-month integration project. Built from scratch.
  • deploy.midnight Releases at 11pm because daytime is too risky. Rollbacks at 2am.
  • security.surface Every new integration is a new attack vector to defend and audit.
  • recon.manual Month-end reconciliation in spreadsheets. Four thousand rows. Weekends consumed.
  • capacity.drain Your engineering team is in maintenance mode, not shipping the products that win business.

Why this works: the dotted-namespace tag (timeout.outage) signals engineering credibility. The body sentence is editorial. Bridges both audiences. Risk: the tag syntax is a deliberate technical signal — non-engineering buyers may find it slightly opaque.

F5

Inline italic editorial

All eight pains as one flowing italic paragraph, with each pain's label in bold crimson. Reads like prose, not a list. Most premium / restrained — closest to an essay or op-ed in a financial publication.

The Problem

You already know the pattern.

Timeouts your customers see before your dashboards do. Reversals that fail silently, found by finance days later. Audits that consume your team for a week. Corridors that take six months to launch — every time. Deploys at midnight, rollbacks at two. Surface area that grows with every new operator. Reconciliation that lives in spreadsheets and weekends. Engineers in maintenance mode, not shipping products.

Why this works: most editorial / publication-grade. Reads in 20 seconds. Crimson labels create natural scan-points. Risk: a wall of italic text may feel uncomfortable for buyers who prefer scannable bullets.

F6

Dictionary entry

Each pain rendered as a dictionary entry: serif headword, crimson chip showing the impact category, italic definition. Numbered down the left. Authoritative, almost reference-grade — but with editorial warmth.

The Problem

The bank-payment lexicon — every term you already know.

01
timeout customer-visible

When your customers know the outage before your dashboards do. Twitter sees it first. Monitoring catches up seven minutes later.

02
silent reversal financial loss

A reversal that fails without firing an alert. Found by finance during reconciliation, three days later. Customer is already gone.

03
week-long audit recurring

"Show me Q3 reversals by reason code." One engineer. Five dashboards. Five days. Every quarter.

04
corridor build six months

A fresh integration project for every new operator. Same protocols, same AML, same audit trail — rebuilt each time.

05
midnight deploy on-call risk

A release scheduled for 11pm because daytime is too risky. Rollback at 2am. On-call paged. Engineering trust erodes.

06
breach surface grows linearly

Every new operator is a new attack vector. Ten operators = ten places encryption can be wrong — and ten audit findings every year.

07
manual reconciliation weekends

Month-end. Spreadsheets. Four thousand rows. Manual matching. A reconciliation incident becomes a weekend war room.

08
engineering drain opportunity cost

Eighty percent of your team's time keeps payments alive. Twenty percent goes to the products that actually win business.

Why this works: most premium feel of the six. The "lexicon" framing turns the pains into named, recognised concepts — words a CTO would use. Crimson impact chips give scannable severity. Risk: longest to read of the six (~40s) but most memorable.
